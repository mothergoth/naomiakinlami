Investigative · Policy · Labor · 2025

A Region of Contradictions

On the Texas Rio Grande Valley, invisible labor, and a data system designed to keep it that way.

There is a different type of hunger in the Texas Rio Grande Valley (RGV), where endless acres of citrus, cotton, sorghum, maize, and vegetables are grown — tended to by some of America's hungriest farmers: undocumented migrants.

"The berries in breakfast cereals, the grains on Thanksgiving tables, and the fast-food bowls consumed daily remain inexpensive because of the small hands that harvest them."

The RGV's agricultural production was valued at $663 million in 2021. In the same year, 143,363 of the 574,295 surveyed households were living below the poverty line. A single large diversified farm — like Rio Fresh Inc. — can generate $147 million annually. The farmworkers who make this possible earn between $20,000–$24,999 a year.

This thesis examines how that contradiction is sustained — through child labor exemptions that date to the 1938 Fair Labor Standards Act, through colonias without running water or paved roads, through census undercounting that starves communities of federal funding, and through a statistical architecture at the USDA and BLS that was designed, from its inception, to render this labor force invisible.

It includes original analysis of Governor Abbott's Operation Lone Star fentanyl claims — which, when cross-referenced against CBP seizure data and U.S. Sentencing Commission reports, unravel entirely. 83.5% of federally sentenced fentanyl traffickers are U.S. citizens. Operation Lone Star accounts for 8% of border seizures. The invasion narrative is arithmetic fiction.

Thesis submitted to the University of Texas at Austin, Spring 2025. Not yet publicly available.

Anatomy of a Liar

On Joseph Awuah-Darko aka Okuntakinte and the pitfalls of Instagram-friendly mental illness.

At first glance, Awuah-Darko's anesthetized Instagram page reads like the foregone Tumblr profile of a teenage girl fresh off an episode of Skins — an entirely choreographed melancholy sealed and sold to the hungry public. What begins as cringe-worthy teenage angst solidifies itself into the cultural zeitgeist as a man addicted to his reflection.

"Designer misery packaged in Alaïa, oblivious to the damage of romanticized decay."

Awuah-Darko boasts 543,000 followers and a brand — FUI (Fucked Up Individuals) — selling €1,500 distressed jackets while claiming to be an "apprehended illegal alien in Poland" sleeping on trains in Amsterdam. Outlets including the Volkskrant and the New York Times have confirmed he is the heir to one of Ghana's wealthiest families.

This piece documents the grift in full: the virtual dinners he charged for and ghosted, the hospice patients whose privacy he violated for content, Anthony Bourdain's suicide repurposed as a storytelling prop, and a follower's private note shared publicly as aesthetic material.

Joseph Awuah-Darko is not a safe place. He is a man who weaponizes the tragedy of others from an emotionally removed distance. For many people, help is not a performance — it's a lifeline. This is predation in a designer trench coat.

Published on Digi Yard, August 2025.

Freedom is Hard to Bear

Free speech and the university — an appraisal.

An Appraisal of Speech

On Freedom of Speech, one must first define freedom, as the constitution does. The First Amendment to the Constitution of the United States prevents Congress from making laws prohibiting the exercise or abridgment of the freedom of speech, reflecting a concept of freedom as a negative liberty—freedom from external constraints, particularly governmental interference. Under this framework, speech is free so long as the state does not prohibit it. While extensive, it provides no context for positive liberties—freedom to speak autonomously—without socioeconomic constraints.

I further contend that there are two classes of constraints to freedom: active and passive. Active constraints are imposed by institutions and systems—financial obligations such as rent and credit, or legislation that governs behavior. Passive constraints, by contrast, are socially and psychologically derived: the desire for legitimacy, agency, and purpose. Presented as choices—pancakes or cereal, art or photography, University of Texas or UVA—passive constraints belie subjection. Yet even these choices carry consequences. Active constraints, however, allow no such flexibility. Given the inevitability of both active and passive constraints, freedom—like freedom of speech—cannot exist in an absolute form and is therefore, in theory, unachievable.

The primary danger to free speech lies not in legislative suppression but social conformity, where one's need for acceptance and the fear of ostracization serve as immediate disciplines to expression. John Stuart Mill describes this as the "tyranny of the majority…the tendency of society to impose…its own ideas and practices as rules of conduct on those who dissent from them." Mill concurs that conformity imposed upon individuals poses a greater threat to free expression than governmental authority, as it operates psychologically—shaping what individuals are willing to think, say, or withhold long before the threat of formal punishment occurs.

The dilemma then lies in the individual's willingness to undertake the consequences of free speech, even as they possess the legislative liberty to expression. Similarly, James Baldwin observes in The Fire Next Time: "I have met only a very few people…who had any real desire to be free. Freedom is hard to bear." Baldwin highlights the sacrificial nature of freedom, suggesting that most people have no desire to be free.

The University

It is certainly the case in my experience that speech is compartmental. In my consideration of an idea deserving wider recognition, I was apprehensive about causing offense, aware that even a carefully articulated position could provoke argument. My hesitation reflects a larger problem: when universities prioritize external parties over fostering inquiry, students and faculty are discouraged from free expression. I am therefore of the opinion that when a university is too malleable to external pressures, it loses the meaningful distinction that separates higher education from secondary schooling.

This modest opinion was designed for an audience educated and employed by the institution—The University of Texas. I composed three main questions: "What is the primary purpose of a university? When does the university fail in its purpose? Can there ever truly be free speech within a university?"

As an act of self-preservation, I initially withheld my opinion at the start of the discussion. In silence, I could avoid the consequences and judgement for inflammatory, faulty, or absurd expression. Like Baldwin, I recognize that freedom is difficult to bear. Even this restrained opinion, chosen for its safety, did not alleviate my anxiety—showing how social context can implicitly delimit discourse.

Keith Whittington articulates this dynamic in his critique of cancel culture on college campuses. He points to Princeton University's small class sizes and dense networking opportunities, arguing that students' inter-reliance and desire to maintain a beneficial social network can function as a mechanism of self-censorship. He contrasts this with faculty experience, noting that a professor can deliver a lecture, express a controversial opinion, and leave without necessarily encountering the same audience again.

Whittington's assertion that close social networks discourage dissent, though poignant, ignores underlying realities of hyper connectivity. Historically, conversations have gone largely unmonitored, preserved if at all through oral recollection, affording individuals a degree of privacy in their expressions. In contrast, the contemporary environment of social media, covert recording devices, and instantaneous publication has rendered speech retrievable, broadcastable, and easily weaponized.

What Mill's treatise on freedom does not consider, however, is the conceptual advent of "words that wound." As articulated by Mari Matsuda and her colleagues, certain forms of discourse can produce tangible somatic effects, complicating the assumption that speech is merely expressive and materially inconsequential. Berkeley law professor John A. Powell argues that classical justifications of free speech are increasingly challenged by contemporary students who seek conditions on expression "given what we know now about stereotype threat and trauma and P.T.S.D."

The discussion concluded that universities must change with the world, as instrumental institutions, they inevitably reflect and are shaped by the majority.

Freedom is Hard to Bear

In my freshman year, I was recommended a book, On Freedom, by Maggie Nelson. Nelson details the fragility of freedom, politics of care, and constraint. To her work, I made the following note: "There is a sense that the world is changing, moving, and so should we. It is perhaps recycling the same processes. A series of constraints we are yet to comprehend. If I am only an organism—a series of unfortunate events. Then that is all I need to be. Why does that bear discomfort?"

In writing this essay I have found an oblique answer: it bears discomfort because freedom is hard to bear. Our individual freedoms do little in protecting us from the freedom of others; only through protest and policing can we extend the bounds of our freedom—and in doing so, inevitably restrict that of others.

It is my opinion that what we desire is not freedom of speech, but freedom from its consequences—freedom for which there exists no legislative framework.

Submitted to the UT Austin Undergraduate Free Speech Essay Contest, 2026.

Assemblage: Cultural Identity Dynamics in Immigration

On migrating from a racially homogenous Nigeria to the United States — and what happens to identity in between.

The acculturative process of migrating from Nigeria — within the context of a united African self-identification — to the United States was shaped by encounters, self-identification, and intersectionality. In Nigeria, identity is ethnic before it is national: among 250 ethnic groups, I existed as Yoruba, a member of a majority group, largely unaware of racialized limitation.

"Pre-encounter was marked by my identity comfort prior to migration. Encounter occurred through multiple pivotal interactions — including a man shouting at my family to 'dress American or go back,' while we wore traditional Yoruba clothing after church."

This essay applies Cross's Nigrescence Model to map early identity disruption, then moves into the Cultural Identity Model to examine how in-group and out-group compartmentalization shaped community building in adolescence — the deliberate curation of Nigerian-American peers as a response to experiences of otherness.

The piece concludes with Ethnocultural Identity theory, which accounts for the immigrant experience more fully than earlier models: acknowledging that identity is not solely race or ethnicity, but the degree of self-identification — and that the pressures of migration and acculturation, when they exceed coping resources, have real psychosocial consequences.

Written for PSY 364T: Multicultural Psychology, University of Texas at Austin, 2024.
